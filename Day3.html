Day3 - Codecademy - Tutorial Hell?

After gaining a solid grounding in HTML over the past couple of days, I was ready to take the next step in my coding journey. My curiosity led me to Codecademy, a popular platform for learning coding interactively. The site offered a structured way to learn, with a combination of theory, exercises, and quizzes. I thought it would be the perfect resource to continue building on my HTML knowledge. However, as I spent more time working through the lessons, I started to feel like I was getting stuck in something I had heard a lot about — "tutorial hell."

The Allure of Codecademy

When I first logged into Codecademy, I was impressed by its organised structure and user-friendly design. The interactive lessons helped me grasp the basic concepts quickly. I was introduced to new elements of HTML and CSS, including more advanced topics like classes, IDs, and the structure of a webpage. The exercises were great for reinforcing the theory, and I felt like I was learning more about how different tags and styles worked together.

At first, it was motivating. I'd complete a module, get a sense of accomplishment from the small wins, and then move on to the next lesson. However, as I continued through the lessons, a pattern started to emerge. While the theory and exercises were useful, I wasn't retaining the knowledge in the way I had hoped. I found myself simply following along with the steps, ticking off boxes without truly understanding the reasoning behind them.

Falling into the Trap of Tutorial Hell

I soon realised that I had fallen into what many developers call "tutorial hell." This term refers to the frustrating cycle where you constantly follow tutorials and exercises without ever going beyond them. It's easy to get stuck in a loop of completing tutorial after tutorial, but without applying the knowledge to real projects, you aren't truly learning

At that point, I realised something crucial: While Codecademy had a wealth of information, it wasn't giving me the opportunity to explore the material independently. I wasn't able to take what I was learning and create something of my own. I wasn't facing the challenges of solving problems on my own — I was just repeating what the tutorial told me to do.

I understood the steps, but I didn't fully comprehend how or why I was using them in context. The knowledge wasn't becoming ingrained, and I felt frustrated. I was learning how to code, but I wasn't learning how to think like a coder.

The Need for Guided Projects

It became clear to me that what I needed was a guided project — something that would teach me the basics but then challenge me to go off on my own and solve problems independently. The idea of working through a project where I could apply my knowledge, test my skills, and confront my weaknesses sounded like the perfect solution.

A guided project would allow me to see the big picture, understand how HTML, CSS, and JavaScript fit together, and create something tangible. After completing a guided project, I'd be better equipped to strike out on my own and continue experimenting, making mistakes, and learning from them.

So, I began my search for such a project. I needed a balance of structure and independence — something to help me get started and then let me take the reins. What I really craved was a task that didn't hold my hand every step of the way, but also didn't leave me in the dark without direction. A project that would let me learn by doing, rather than just by following.

Shifting my Focus

While Codecademy was certainly useful, I realised I needed to strike a better balance between guided learning and hands-on practice. I decided to take a step back from the exercises and focus on creating a small project — a personal webpage or a simple website design, something manageable but with enough freedom to make it my own. I wanted to see how the theory I had learned could translate into actual design and development.

The goal was simple: I would start with a basic layout, like a portfolio or blog page, and use the knowledge I had gained so far to create the structure. But I wouldn't rely solely on tutorials. I would work through the challenges myself, using my cheat sheet and other resources only when I got stuck. This would force me to apply my knowledge and fill in the gaps where I had fallen short.

What I Learned

Day 3 brought a lot of clarity. I learned that learning to code isn't just about following along with tutorials — it's about applying what you learn to real-world projects. Here's what I took away from the day:

  1. Tutorials are useful, but they aren't enough It's easy to get caught up in the comfort of following step-by-step instructions. While tutorials can teach you syntax and basic principles, real learning happens when you apply your knowledge to solve problems on your own.
  2. Guided projects offer the right balance: The best way to move forward is by finding projects that provide a bit of structure but also challenge you to think critically and work independently. A guided project can help you build confidence and reinforce what you've learned.
  3. Learning is messy: It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking that learning is a smooth, linear process. But in reality, it's full of trial and error. The more I struggled through challenges and mistakes, the more I began to understand how things worked.
  4. The importance of independence: The real learning comes when you stop relying on others to tell you what to do and start figuring things out yourself. It's through this process of problem-solving that you begin to internalise the skills you need.

Looking Ahead

Day 3 was a wake-up call. While Codecademy offered a lot of useful information, it wasn't enough to build the skills I needed to truly move forward. I'm now more focused on working through projects that allow me to apply my knowledge, even if they involve some trial and error. I'm looking for projects that give me the basics and then let me go off on my own, face challenges, and work through my mistakes.

It's clear that in order to truly learn, I need to move beyond just following along and start building something real. I'm excited to see how these next projects help me grow as a developer — and I'm ready to take on the next challenge.

Day 3 was an important step in my coding journey. I've realised that tutorials have their place, but the real learning begins when you start building and problem-solving on your own. I'm ready to take the next leap, and I can't wait to see what I'll create with the knowledge I've gained so far.
